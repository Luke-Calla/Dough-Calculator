How the Pizza Dough Calculator Works

A plain-English guide to what the calculator uses, what it estimates, and what it leaves to your judgement.

How to Use the Calculator

01

Choose the base

Pick a pizza style, then set how many dough balls you want and how heavy each ball should be.

02

Choose the leavener

Use instant dry yeast, fresh yeast, or sourdough starter. The calculator shows the fields that matter for that choice.

03

Set time and temperature

Enter your room time, fridge time, room temperature, fridge temperature, and target bake time.

04

Read the formula

The output gives ingredient weights and a schedule counted back from when you want to bake.

Baker's Percentages

Baker's percentages start with flour. Flour is always 100%, and everything else is measured against that flour weight.

This is why the recipe scales cleanly. Change the number of pizzas or the dough-ball weight, and the calculator can rebuild the ingredient weights while keeping the same dough style.

Example formula
Flour 100%
Water 65%

Oil and sugar 0%
Your selected style and overrides decide the actual values.

Pizza Style Presets

Presets are starting points. They set the default ball weight, hydration, salt, oil, and sugar for a style, but you can still change the values that matter to your bake.

Leavener Modes

IDY

IDY amounts can look tiny because they are based on flour weight. Longer ferments usually need less yeast.

Time and Temperature

Fermentation depends on both time and temperature. A warm room moves dough along faster; a cold fridge slows it down.

The calculator uses your room and fridge settings to estimate the leavener amount for the schedule you want.

If the dough goes into the fridge, the calculator can also add a pull-from-fridge step and estimate a warm-up window before baking.

Bake Schedule

The schedule starts with your bake time and works backwards. From there it subtracts warm-up time, fridge time, room time, and sourdough starter timing when needed.

A room-only dough may only need mix and bake steps. A cold ferment adds fridge steps. Sourdough adds a starter feed before the mix.

What It Does Not Know

The calculator gives you a useful plan, but it cannot see the dough. It does not know your flour strength, starter health, actual dough temperature, room-temperature swings, humidity, handling, or oven setup.

Warm-up time is an estimate. Auto warm-up can be zero when the dough has not chilled enough to need a separate pull-from-fridge step.
